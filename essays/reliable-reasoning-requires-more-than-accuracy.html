Reliable Reasoning Requires More Than Accuracy

Accuracy tells us whether a model got the answer right. Reliability asks whether we know when to trust it.

Jae Oh Woo

The default way to evaluate a language model is accuracy: what fraction of the time does it produce the correct answer? This metric is useful, but it is dangerously incomplete. A model that is right 90% of the time but gives no signal about which 10% it gets wrong is, in many contexts, less useful than a model that is right 80% of the time but reliably knows when it is uncertain.

Silent failures
Users can't distinguish good from bad outputs

Why 80% with calibration can outperform 90% without

The Calibration Gap

A well-calibrated model is one whose confidence scores correspond to actual probabilities. When it says it is 90% confident, it should be correct roughly 90% of the time. Most modern LLMs are poorly calibrated — they express high confidence even when wrong, and their uncertainty estimates are unreliable.

This is not merely an aesthetic problem. In any system where a human or downstream process must decide whether to act on a model's output, calibration is essential. Without it, the user has no basis for distinguishing reliable outputs from unreliable ones.

A confident wrong answer is not just useless — it is actively harmful, because it erodes the trust needed for AI systems to be useful at all.

Why Accuracy Benchmarks Miss the Point

Standard benchmarks report aggregate accuracy across a test set. But aggregate performance hides distributional structure. A model might achieve 95% accuracy overall while systematically failing on logical negation, multi-step arithmetic, or causal reasoning under confounders.

Worse, optimizing for benchmark accuracy can actively harm reliability. Models learn to exploit surface patterns in benchmark datasets, producing correct answers for the wrong reasons. This inflates accuracy while making the model more brittle on out-of-distribution inputs.

Levels of trustworthiness in AI systems — from weakest to strongest

At the bottom, we have raw accuracy — the weakest form of evaluation. Each layer adds a stronger form of reliability: soft confidence, selective prediction, calibrated uncertainty, and at the top, formal statistical guarantees that bound the probability of error.

From Prediction to Uncertainty Estimation

The path toward reliable reasoning requires treating uncertainty as a first-class output. Several frameworks make this possible:

Bayesian approaches model uncertainty through posterior distributions. In our work on analytic mutual information in BNNs, we showed that closed-form uncertainty expressions are achievable, removing the need for expensive Monte Carlo approximation.

Proxy-based methods estimate uncertainty without modifying the base model. Our ICML 2024 work demonstrated that proxy-based uncertainty estimation can significantly improve instruction following — the model learns when to trust its own outputs.

VC-theoretic analysis provides distribution-free bounds on the gap between observed and true performance. Our NeurIPS 2025 work applies this to LLM self-evaluation, answering the question: when can a model reliably judge its own outputs?

Key Insight

Reliability should not be bolted on after the fact. It should be a design principle from the beginning — in architecture, training, and evaluation.

The Practical Stakes

When a language model assists in medical diagnosis, legal analysis, or scientific reasoning, the cost of a confident but wrong answer can be severe. The goal is not to make models that never make mistakes — that is unrealistic — but to make models that communicate their uncertainty honestly, so that humans and systems downstream can make informed decisions about when to trust and when to verify.

Accuracy is necessary but not sufficient. Reliability is the harder, more important goal.
