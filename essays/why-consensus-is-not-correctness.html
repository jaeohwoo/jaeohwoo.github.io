Why Consensus Is Not Correctness in Multi-Agent AI

Multi-agent systems can agree on the wrong answer. Why uncertainty control matters more than surface-level agreement.

Jae Oh Woo

There is a natural intuition that if multiple agents agree, the answer is probably right. This is the logic behind ensemble methods, committee voting, and more recently, multi-agent debate in large language models. But agreement is not the same as correctness, and conflating the two can produce confidently wrong systems.

Consider a panel of LLMs debating a factual question. After several rounds, they converge on a single answer. Does convergence mean they found the truth? Not necessarily. If the models share similar training distributions, similar biases, and similar failure modes, they can easily converge on the same error. Consensus amplifies shared knowledge, but it also amplifies shared blind spots.

The Problem with Majority Rules

Majority voting is appealing because it is simple. But simplicity comes at a cost. When agents share correlated errors — which is almost always the case for models from similar training paradigms — majority voting provides weaker guarantees than we might hope. The effective diversity of the ensemble is lower than the number of agents suggests.

A room full of people trained on the same textbook will agree confidently on whatever that textbook got wrong.

This is not a hypothetical concern. In practice, LLM-based multi-agent systems routinely exhibit correlated failures on tasks requiring reasoning outside the distribution of common training data. The agents agree, but they agree on something wrong.

From Agreement to Calibrated Uncertainty

The alternative is to move from asking "what do most agents think?" to asking "how uncertain is the system, and can we control the risk of being wrong?" This is where conformal prediction becomes valuable.

Conformal methods do not try to pick a single answer. Instead, they produce prediction sets — collections of possible answers that are guaranteed to contain the true answer with a user-specified probability. The guarantee is distribution-free: it holds regardless of the underlying model or data distribution.

Two approaches to multi-agent aggregation

Applied to multi-agent deliberation, this means we can let agents debate and aggregate their views, but instead of forcing a single consensus answer, we produce a set of answers with formal coverage guarantees. If the set is small, the system is genuinely confident. If the set is large, the system is honestly uncertain — and this uncertainty signal is itself valuable for downstream decision-making.

Why This Matters for Safety

In safety-critical applications — medical diagnosis, legal reasoning, scientific discovery — a confidently wrong answer is far more dangerous than an honestly uncertain one.

Key Insight

The right question is not "do the agents agree?" but "can we bound the probability that the system's output is wrong?" These are fundamentally different questions, and they lead to fundamentally different system designs.

The Conformal Social Choice Framework

In our recent work, we formalize this idea through conformal social choice — a framework that combines social choice theory (how to aggregate preferences) with conformal prediction (how to provide statistical guarantees). The key innovation is treating multi-agent deliberation not as a voting problem, but as an uncertainty quantification problem.

Looking Forward

As multi-agent AI systems become more prevalent — in code generation, research assistance, autonomous decision-making — the need for principled uncertainty control will only grow. Consensus is a useful signal, but it is not a substitute for statistical guarantees. The systems we trust with important decisions should be the ones that know what they don't know.
